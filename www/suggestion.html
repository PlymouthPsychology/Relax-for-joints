Using positive imagery and suggestion
> What we expect to happen can have a strong influence our experience. This is particularly true for pain. Pain can seem simple: it's the sensation we feel when we injure ourselves. But pain is ***not*** this simple. To experience pain, we need more than just signals from the body. We also need to interpret these signals as *dangerous*. This is why relaxing can help reduce pain: when we relax we find bodily sensations less threatening, and less painful.
Changing expectations and experience
There are many ways to change our expectations, and therefore our experience of pain. One common technique is to think of an outcome we would like (for example, for our pain to slowly subside), and focus our attention on this. Imagining things doesn't always make them come true â€” if it did we would all be lottery winners! But our brains and bodies are tightly connected and have a strong influence on each other. Many scientific studies have shown that pain and nausea can be reduced by a substantial amount in this way. > We are able to change how we experience pain, sensations from our body, and how we move, by listening and responding to positive suggestions.
Audio recordings of positive suggestions
